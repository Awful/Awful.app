Helsing posted:

It matters a great deal in terms of national living standards whether your country exists within the core of the world economy vs. being on the periphery. Sure generally speaking this is true. It's not clear we had that option, but if we were more central to the global economy we would benefit from that.

quote:

We also ought to be cautious about declaring that the capitalist system has entered an irreversible decline. Similar predictions made in the 1870s, during the First World War, during the Depression, following World War II, and again during the 1970s. While you could argue that in many ways the problems afflicting us now are actually a continuation of unresolved problems from the crisis of the 1970s, none of the other listed eras have produced the global collapse of capitalism that Marxists hope for.

I don't think capitalism is in irreversable decline right now (Neither do most Marxists). As you say, it is experiencing "unresolved problems." If there was a massive devaluation of capital through a giant economic crash or a world war there would be a huge resurgence of profitability that would probably give capitalism another 20 year "golden age." Capitalism could also regain some vitality by developing Africa.

quote:

Or, even more unpredictably, scientists unlocate some new phenomenon in nature that can be exploited. We find a cheap source of energy, we unlock a powerful new technology like electrification, we make a breakthrough advance in chemistry that unlocks previously unimagined yields in agriculture.

While it would be foolish to assume that we'll have such a breakthrough, it isn't sensible to rule one out. Whether the innovations are political or technological there are all kinds of unexpected events that could restore the viability of the capitalist system.

Its true that these Schumpeterian solutions CAN ameliorate the situation, but sometimes (as in the computer revolution) they actually make it worse.

quote:

Even if we restricted ourselves to a traditional Marxist interpretation, deteriorating global conditions could produce a much deeper depression than the one we've experienced since 2007, doing immense damage to the economy but ultimately clearing the decks to restore profitability. Based on my understanding of Marx there's no particular reason to think the present crisis should be the last one, since once the depression gets deep enough it should destroy enough capital stock to restore profitability.

Yes I agree with this. Assuming there is a capitalism left to recover it would experience a massive boom in profitability. The current practice of "kicking the can down the road" through debt extensions is preventing this from happening, and it is not being done because the prospect of another great depression is too terrifying to the ruling class. As capitalism expands in scale and integrates more and more of the world, crashes like those become more and more frightening. Therefore capitalism is "stagnant" at this time.

quote:

Financialization and globalization were, as I see it, a reaction to a couple of factors.

None of this seems incompatible with the argument that the rise of finance capital was bad for the long term health of the system. In most cases the transition to financialization made a lot of sense for each individual actor, but individually rational decisions often aggregate into undesirable global outcomes. In the case of financialization and globalization it seems as though this may at least partially be the case.

I basically agree with what you're saying here. The fallout of the 1970s crisis made investing in finance more attractive. However some recent research suggests that the shift to financialization may be overstated (relative industrial capital investment has not declined as much as is commonly thought).

quote:

This is indeed a likely outcome. You say that "milquetoast postwar Social Democracy" and "liberal solutions" won't work, so please explain to me how SOME FORM of socialization of the means of production (The dictatorship of the proletariat is a slippery term, it could mean a lot of different things) is not required in order to prevent the possibility of a fascist dystopia.

quote:

Finally, I'd argue that even if we stick with a Marxist interpretation, there's a very strong case to be made that Marx was a de facto parliamentary socialist, and that if you read his more considered academic theorizing there's a strong basis for concluding that capitalism as he models it could continue uninterrupted for many centuries until it actually socialzies the means of production, a process that is still highly incomplete. There's no reason to be confident that the world is actually ready for the kind of socialism Marx anticipated. This is highly disputed but it is certainly one possible interpretation of Marx. I personally think that Marx was neither entirely for or against parliament. He had reservations about party politics and he attacked the social democrats of his time for "parliamentary cretinism" suggests that he was against relying too much on parliament as the main arena of politics. I therefore think he was in favour of a combination of parliamentary and extra-parliamentary politics. On the issue of the "final limits to capital" the fact that mainstream economists have started to pick up his arguments about automation and the reduction of labour time from the Grundrisse's Fragment on Machines (Which present a somewhat distinct problem from the falling profit rate issue) suggests that the POSSIBILITY of socialism is not as far off as you suggest. Capitalism can be reduced to a monstrous absurdity and still keep on going. There is no automatic socialism, and capitalism will never "socialize the means of production" itself. It requires a political effort based on the existing conditions to make that happen.

To whatever extent capital does "socialize" itself, it does so through the financial system and the creation of large corporations. In that sense globalization has brought us closer to the possibility of world socialism, not further away from it.
